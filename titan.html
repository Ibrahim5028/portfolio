Titan Submersible Project

About the Project


In our project, we conducted an engineering analysis of the Titan submersible, focusing on its catastrophic implosion during a tourist mission to the Titanic. Our study included evaluating materials, fluid dynamics, geometry, and manufacturing methods. We utilized Finite Element Method (FEM) modeling for predicting diving depth and assessing structural integrity, and Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) for analyzing hydrodynamic resistance during maneuvers.


Additionally, we proposed enhancements to improve performance, validated through simulations. This comprehensive analysis served as an educational resource for our Simulation Tools for Design & Analysis course, highlighting the significance of multidisciplinary approaches in engineering.


I was largely responsible for the FEM analysis and helped with the CFD simulation. Based on our findings we concluded that the submersible's failure was largely due to its design, notably its composite hull combining carbon fiber-reinforced plastic (CFRP) and titanium alloy. The CFRP's susceptibility to defects under deep-sea pressures, along with the stress from multiple dives, played a significant role. Additionally, the construction process, which strayed from standard industry practices, was a key factor in the implosion.


Skill Acquired:


Engineering Analysis, Finite Element Method (FEM) Modeling, Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD), Simulation and Validation
